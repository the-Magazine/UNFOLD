Special Issue: Nigeria's Youth Entrepreneurship Revolution

By Ade Johnson â€¢ May 10, 2023

In the heart of Lagos, Africa's largest city, a quiet revolution is taking place. Young Nigerian entrepreneurs are building startups that are transforming not just Nigeria's economy, but are setting precedents for the entire continent and beyond. This special issue of Unfold Magazine explores the driving forces, challenges, and successes of Nigeria's youth entrepreneurship movement.

The Catalyst of Change

With over 200 million people and more than 60% of the population under the age of 25, Nigeria possesses an enormous demographic dividend. Young Nigerians, faced with limited employment opportunities in traditional sectors, have increasingly turned to entrepreneurship as both a necessity and an opportunity for innovation.

"We're witnessing a fundamental shift in mindset," explains Tolu Adesina, founder of TechNigeria, a Lagos-based incubator. "Young Nigerians no longer wait for government or corporations to create jobs. They're creating their own opportunities and, in turn, employment for others."

Technology as the Great Equalizer

The tech sector has become the most visible face of this entrepreneurial wave. Companies like Flutterwave, Paystack, and Andela have achieved international recognition and significant funding. Flutterwave, a payments technology company founded by young Nigerian entrepreneurs, reached unicorn status in 2021 with a valuation exceeding $1 billion.

"Technology has democratized entrepreneurship," says Ezra Olubi, co-founder of Paystack, which was acquired by Stripe for over $200 million. "You can build a global company from Lagos with relatively little initial capital. The barriers that held back previous generations are falling."

Beyond Technology: Diverse Entrepreneurial Fields

While tech startups grab headlines, young Nigerian entrepreneurs are making impacts across diverse sectors. In fashion, designers like Adebayo Oke-Lawal of Orange Culture and Amaka Osakwe of Maki Oh have gained international recognition, showcasing Nigerian creativity on global platforms including London and New York Fashion Weeks.

In agriculture, entrepreneurs are developing innovative solutions to improve efficiency and reduce waste. Farmcrowdy, founded by Onyeka Akumah, has pioneered an agricultural investment platform that connects small-scale farmers with investors, providing farmers with necessary funds, technical support, and access to markets.

The Challenges They Face

Despite their successes, young Nigerian entrepreneurs encounter significant challenges. Unreliable infrastructure, particularly electricity, remains a major obstacle. Many entrepreneurs report spending up to 30% of their operational budgets on power generation. Access to capital is another persistent challenge, with traditional banking systems often reluctant to fund startups.

"Nigerian entrepreneurs have to be exceptionally resilient," notes Kola Aina, founding partner at Ventures Platform, an early-stage fund for African digital entrepreneurs. "They're building world-class businesses despite infrastructure and funding challenges that entrepreneurs in more developed markets don't face."

Government's Evolving Role

The Nigerian government's approach to the entrepreneurial ecosystem has evolved over time. Recent initiatives like the Youth Enterprise with Innovation in Nigeria (YouWiN) program and the establishment of technology hubs in major cities represent steps toward a more supportive environment.

However, many entrepreneurs believe more can be done. "We need policies that make it easier to start and operate businesses," argues Olumide Soyombo, co-founder of Bluechip Technologies. "This includes tax incentives for startups, simplified regulatory processes, and greater investment in digital infrastructure."

The Global Impact

Nigerian entrepreneurship is increasingly influential on the global stage. Diaspora Nigerians play significant roles in international tech companies, while homegrown startups are expanding their operations beyond Nigeria's borders.

"What we're seeing now is just the beginning," predicts Iyinoluwa Aboyeji, co-founder of Flutterwave and Andela. "Nigerian entrepreneurs are building solutions that address both local and global challenges. In the coming decade, I believe we'll see Nigerian companies become household names internationally."

The Future Outlook

As Nigeria continues to navigate economic challenges, its young entrepreneurs represent a beacon of hope and a powerful force for transformation. Their innovation, resilience, and determination are reshaping the nation's economic landscape and inspiring a new generation of business leaders across Africa.

With increasing international investment, improving infrastructure, and a growing recognition of the potential of Nigeria's entrepreneurial ecosystem, the future looks promising. The revolution has begun, and its impact will likely be felt for generations to come.
